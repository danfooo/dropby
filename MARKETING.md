# dropby — Marketing Checklist

Everything to think about when it comes to getting dropby into people's hands, keeping them, and helping it spread.

---

## How people find out it exists

- Word of mouth (friend tells friend)
- App Store search (someone types "hangout app")
- Google search (someone types "how to see friends more")
- Social media content (TikTok, Reels, Shorts)
- Paid social ads (Instagram, TikTok, Facebook)
- Press coverage (tech blogs, lifestyle publications, local papers)
- Influencer mentions (paid or organic)
- AI assistant answers (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity)
- Product directories (Product Hunt, AlternativeTo, app review sites)
- Reddit / forum posts (authentic, not spammy)
- Podcast mentions or interviews
- University bulletin boards, community boards, coworking spaces
- App store features (Apple and Google editorially curate "apps of the day")
- Cross-promotion with complementary apps (not competitors)
- QR codes on physical things (stickers, posters, coasters at cafes)

## What you say (messaging)

- The one-sentence pitch (for when someone asks "what's dropby?")
- The emotional hook (the feeling, not the feature)
- The positioning (what mental category it goes in: not a calendar, not a chat, not a social network)
- Handling the main objection ("why do I need an app for this?")
- Tone of voice (warm, short, conversational, no marketing-speak)
- The name and how you use it (always lowercase, always "dropby" not "Dropby")
- Copy consistency across every surface (app store, website, in-app, emails, social)
- Avoiding words that trigger the wrong association ("invite," "schedule," "network," "feed")

## Where the message lives (surfaces)

- The website landing page
- The `/story` page (shareable emotional narrative)
- App Store listing (title, subtitle, description, keywords)
- Google Play listing (same, different rules)
- App Store screenshots and preview video
- Open Graph tags (what shows when someone shares a dropby link on WhatsApp, iMessage, Slack)
- Social media profiles (bio, pinned post, profile picture)
- Email templates (verification, invite, nudge, waitlist)
- Push notification copy
- The in-app onboarding flow
- A blog or content section on the website
- A `/llms.txt` file for AI assistants
- Structured data on the site (schema.org markup so Google and AI understand what dropby is)

## The path from hearing about it to using it (the funnel)

- Awareness (they know it exists)
- Interest (they look closer)
- Install (they download it)
- Signup (they create an account)
- First friend added (the app stops being empty)
- First door opened (they've done the core action)
- First "going" received (someone responded)
- First real meetup (the app delivered on its promise)
- Repeat use (they come back next week)
- Referral (they bring someone else in)
- Every step between these loses people, and each drop-off has a different cause and fix

## Making it spread (virality)

- Viral coefficient (does each user bring in at least one more?)
- The invite link (one tap to share, pre-connects the friendship)
- The shareable story (something you can text a friend that does the convincing for you)
- The door notification as a built-in reminder that the app exists
- Shareable moments ("I opened my door, come over" as something people post)
- Social proof (showing that real meetups are happening, even small numbers)
- Healthy FOMO (a friend is hanging out and you see it, so you want to go)
- Network effects (more friends on it means more doors to see)
- The ambassador's toolkit (what does the person recommending it have to work with?)
- Making it easy for ambassadors to explain without sounding like they're pitching
- Protecting the ambassador's reputation (the product must deliver on what they promised)

## Getting people to stick (retention)

- The first-week experience (if nothing happens in 7 days, they're gone)
- Gentle nudges (reminders to open the door, not spam)
- Seasonal prompts ("who do you want to see more this summer?")
- The two-week rule (a real meetup needs to happen within 14 days)
- Reminders tied to specific days and times the user has been active before
- Post-meetup celebration (acknowledge when something real happened)
- Making it effortless to add more friends over time
- Not annoying people into uninstalling (every notification must feel useful, not needy)

## Search and discoverability (SEO and ASO)

- Keywords people actually search for ("see friends more," "spontaneous hangout")
- Content on your domain that matches those searches (articles, guides, the story page)
- App store keywords, title, and subtitle optimization
- App store ratings and reviews (directly affect ranking)
- Backlinks from other sites (press, blogs, directories linking to dropby.app)
- Page speed and mobile-friendliness (Google ranking factors)
- Sitemap and robots.txt
- Local SEO if relevant (city-specific content)

## Getting into AI answers

- Being present in AI training data (press, Product Hunt, app directories, Wikipedia eventually)
- An `/llms.txt` file on your site describing what dropby is
- Structured data (schema.org/SoftwareApplication)
- FAQ content that directly answers questions people ask AIs
- App store descriptions written in natural language (AI models pull from these)
- Being in retrieval sources (ranking in Google helps because AI models with search pull from results)

## Helping ambassadors succeed

- A shareable link they can text (the `/story` page)
- A personal invite link that pre-connects the friendship
- The instant proof moment (ambassador opens door while friend installs)
- Framing suggestions ("I found this digital doorbell thing, want to try Saturday?")
- Tying the ask to a specific moment, not an abstract future benefit
- Giving ambassadors a sense of ownership (early access, direct feedback channel)
- Not turning friendship into a referral program (no points, no rewards, no gamification)

## Trust and credibility

- Privacy posture (what data you collect, what you don't, being upfront about it)
- Design quality (polished app signals serious team)
- App store ratings and reviews
- Real testimonials (specific and concrete, not generic praise)
- Longevity signals (people won't invest in adding friends if the app might disappear)
- Transparency about the team (the About page)

## Timing

- Day of week (Friday afternoon is when people think about weekend plans)
- Seasonal moments (New Year, summer, back to school, holidays)
- Life transitions (moving cities, new job, graduation)
- Cultural moments (loneliness trending in the news, screen time studies)
- Time of day for push notifications and social posts

## Measuring what works

- Attribution (knowing which channel drove each install)
- UTM parameters on links
- App store analytics (where installs come from)
- Conversion rate at each funnel step
- Retention curves (how many people are still active after 1 day, 7 days, 30 days)
- Viral coefficient (installs per user)
- Cost per install (if running paid ads)
- Which messages and hooks perform best (A/B testing)
- Uninstall reasons
- Feedback from ambassadors about what objections they hit

## Localization

- Translating all surfaces (app, website, app store listings, emails)
- Adapting the message culturally (the problem statement hits differently in different places)
- Understanding which markets have the strongest pull (where is the "spontaneous visit" most dead?)
- Local influencers and press in target markets

## Things that are easy to forget

- Onboarding IS marketing (the first 60 seconds determine everything)
- Every email you send is marketing (verification, invite, nudge)
- Every push notification is marketing (useful or annoying, nothing in between)
- The app store screenshots are often the only thing someone sees before deciding
- Open Graph previews matter (what does a dropby link look like in a WhatsApp chat?)
- Compounding channels (SEO, word of mouth) beat one-shot channels (press, viral posts) long term
- The product itself is the best marketing (if meetups happen, people talk about it)
- Negative word of mouth spreads faster than positive (one bad experience gets told to more people than one good one)
